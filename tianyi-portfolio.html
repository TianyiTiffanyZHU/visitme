Thinking

A particular
way of seeing.

"Fragrance cannot be photographed. It lives in memory before it lives in the present. It identifies without announcing itself." — On the phenomenology of scent

My academic formation is in how meaning moves — through language, through social institutions, through the objects we choose to keep near us. A degree in Communication from the University of Macau, followed by a Master's in Philosophy of Social Science at the London School of Economics, gave me a particular way of seeing: not just what people want, but why wanting takes the forms it does.

Luxury beauty, I came to understand, is not merely product. It is a system of meaning — a set of propositions about how one should move through the world, what one deserves, what sensation is worth attending to. The most compelling luxury brands do not sell objects; they offer a coherent philosophy of the good life, and invite the customer to affirm it.

Fragrance, more than any category I know, operates at this register. It cannot be photographed, only approximated. It lives in memory before it lives in the present. It connects the chemical to the cultural, the personal to the universal. A fragrance brief is, in its deepest structure, a philosophical argument — about identity, about provenance, about what endures.

I bring this framework to product marketing. It sharpens everything.

01
Conceptual Rigour

Before a brief is written, the underlying question must be clearly stated. What is this product actually claiming? What world does it presuppose?

02
Commercial Precision

Ideas without execution are indulgences. I work in the tension between depth of vision and the practical demands of campaign, calendar, and P&L.

03
Cultural Sensitivity

Meaning is not universal. What luxury communicates in Shanghai differs from what it communicates in Melbourne. I hold both inside frame simultaneously.

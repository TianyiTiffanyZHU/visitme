Thinking

A particular
way of seeing.

My formation is in how meaning moves — through language, through social institutions, through the objects people choose to keep near them. A degree in Communication from Macau University of Science and Technology, followed by a Master's in Philosophy of Social Science at the London School of Economics, gave me a particular way of seeing: not just what people want, but why wanting takes the forms it does.

Luxury beauty, I came to understand, is not merely product. It is a system of meaning — a set of propositions about how one should move through the world, what one deserves, what sensation is worth attending to. The most compelling luxury brands do not sell objects; they offer a coherent philosophy of the good life, and invite the customer to affirm it.

I bring this framework to product marketing. It sharpens everything.

Dissertation · ~10,000 words
Contrasting Simulations and Experiments in Social Sciences
Materiality Thesis and Epistemic Privilege Revisited

Argues that the Materiality Thesis — the dominant account of what separates computer simulations from experiments — is inarticulate in social science, where the objects of study are often non-physical. Proposes that what truly differentiates the two methods is how each achieves relevant similarity with its target system: simulations through good modelling, experiments through shared kindhood. From a pragmatic perspective, neither method holds epistemic privilege — each produces knowledge the other cannot.

Supervised by Dr Kate Vredenburgh

Essay · ~2,000 words
Negative Freedom and Non-domination
On Berlin, Einspahr, and the Republican Tradition

Defends republican non-domination as the preferable conception of freedom over Berlin's negative liberty. Non-domination extends the scope of what counts as unfreedom by attending to structural constraints — not only direct interference — and motivates the search for enhancement of freedom under non-ideal conditions, where the individualist tradition falls short.
